The Dichotomy of Control

Epictetus's foundational insight: distinguish sharply between what is up to us (our judgments, choices, intentions) and what is not (everything else). This single distinction is the root of Stoic serenity.

The Shadow Archetype

Carl Jung on the shadow: the parts of yourself you deny or repress don't disappear — they project onto others. Integrating the shadow is the essential work of psychological maturity.

The Cave and the Forms

Plato's allegory maps the structure of reality: the shadow-world of appearances, the fire of reason, and the sun of the Good. Most humans live their entire lives mistaking shadows for truth.

Amor Fati — Love of Fate

Nietzsche's formula for the highest possible attitude: not merely accepting fate but loving whatever happens. What would it mean to not merely endure your life but embrace it entirely?

The Stoic View of Wealth

True wealth is not the abundance of possessions but the richness of character. Seneca: "It is not the man who has too little, but the man who craves more, that is poor."

The Three Types of Friendship

Aristotle identified friendships of utility, pleasure, and virtue. Only the third — where both parties wish the excellence of the other — endures. Everything else is conditional and temporary.

The Philosopher-King

Plato's radical proposal: society should be governed not by those who crave power but by those who have been trained to understand reality. Leadership as service, not domination.

The Art of Voluntary Discomfort

The Stoic practice of voluntary hardship — cold showers, fasting, sleeping on the floor — trains the one faculty that matters above all: the ability to be undisturbed by circumstances.

Cognitive Defusion

Modern psychological reframing of the Stoic practice: thoughts are not facts. Observing your thinking — rather than being consumed by it — is the foundation of mental clarity and emotional regulation.
